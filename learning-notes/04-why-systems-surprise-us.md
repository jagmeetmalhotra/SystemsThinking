# Chapter 4 — Why Systems Surprise Us

## The one big idea
Systems constantly blindside us — not because they're random, but because our
*minds* take shortcuts they don't have. We watch events instead of structure,
think in straight lines, draw fake boundaries, ignore stacked limits and delays,
and assume everyone has full information. Fix the thinking, lose the surprise.

## The analogy: driving in thick fog
You can only see a few feet ahead (limited info), the car reacts a moment after
you steer (delay), the road bends in ways a ruler can't predict (nonlinear), and
where "your trip" starts and ends is something you made up (boundaries). Crashes
in fog aren't caused by a bad road — they're caused by driving as if the fog
weren't there.

## Core concepts, plainly — the six ways we get fooled

### 1. We're mesmerized by events
News, stock ticks, today's outage — **events** are the tip of the iceberg. Below
them is **behavior** (the trend over time), and below *that* is **structure**
(the stocks and loops producing it). Reacting event-by-event is like mopping the
floor while the tap runs. *Go down: event → behavior → structure.*

### 2. Linear minds in a nonlinear world
We expect "twice the push → twice the result." Reality is full of
**nonlinearities**: a little fertilizer helps, a lot poisons; a road with a few
extra cars flows fine, then one more car triggers gridlock. Nonlinear
relationships are exactly what make loops *change which one dominates* — so they
are the chief source of surprise.

### 3. There are no separate systems — boundaries are invented
Nothing in reality has a clean edge; everything connects. We *draw* boundaries to
think at all — but then forget we drew them. **Too narrow** and the cause sits
just outside your box (you fix your yard while the flood comes from upstream).
**Too wide** and you drown in detail. The skill is choosing the boundary that
fits *this* question — and being willing to redraw it for the next one.

### 4. Layers of limits
Nothing grows forever; a growing thing is surrounded by **many limits at once**,
and the *most limiting one changes over time*. Fix the current bottleneck and
you don't get infinite growth — you just reach the **next** limit. (Water was
scarce; you solve water; now land is the limit; solve land; now it's clean air.)
**Growth itself changes which limit bites next.** Watch the upcoming limit, not
just today's.

### 5. Delays are everywhere
Between an action and its effect there's almost always a lag — perception delays,
decision delays, delivery delays. Delays make us over- and under-react, causing
overshoot and oscillation. You usually can't remove the important delays, so you
must *respect* them: act earlier, expect the lag, don't yank the wheel.

### 6. Bounded rationality
Everyone makes **reasonable** decisions based on the **limited, delayed
information they actually have** — not the full system view. The fisherman
doesn't know the total catch; the manager doesn't know true demand. So blaming
"bad/stupid people" is almost always wrong. Put a "rational" new person in the
same position with the same information and they'll do the same thing.
> **The fix is structural: change the information people see and the incentives
> they face, and their behavior changes.** "If you want to understand a system's
> behavior, look at the information and rewards the actors are actually getting."

## Where your understanding will crack

1. **Staying at the event level.** Every "let's respond to this incident" with no
   look at behavior/structure keeps you mopping under an open tap.
2. **Assuming proportionality.** "A bit more input → a bit more output" fails
   exactly where it matters — near thresholds and tipping points.
3. **Forgetting you drew the boundary.** Most stuck arguments are really boundary
   disputes in disguise.
4. **Solving one limit and expecting takeoff.** You just promoted the *next*
   limit to bottleneck.
5. **Blaming people instead of structure.** Bounded rationality says: same seat,
   same info → same behavior. Change the seat.

## Stress-test questions
1. A website keeps having outages and the team keeps adding "post-incident fixes"
   but nothing improves. Which level are they stuck at (event/behavior/structure),
   and what should they look at instead?
2. A city widens a highway to cut traffic; a year later it's just as jammed. Name
   two ideas from this chapter that explain why.
3. Two departments blame each other's "incompetent people" for a recurring mess.
   What does *bounded rationality* say is more likely going on — and what would
   you change?

## Key lines from the book
- *"Systems fool us by presenting themselves—or we fool ourselves by seeing the
  world—as a series of events."*
- *"There are no separate systems. The world is a continuum. Where to draw a
  boundary… depends on the purpose of the discussion."*
- *"Bounded rationality means that people make quite reasonable decisions based
  on the information they have."*
