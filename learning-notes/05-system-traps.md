# Chapter 5 — System Traps… and Opportunities

## The one big idea
Some structures reliably produce bad behavior **no matter how smart or
well-meaning the people are**. These are *archetypes* — traps. The good news:
each trap has a known escape, and the escape is almost always **structural**
(change a goal, a rule, or an information flow), never "try harder."

> [`../systems-thinking-skill/references/system-traps.md`](../systems-thinking-skill/references/system-traps.md)

## The analogy: a slippery pit with a rope
Each trap is a differently-shaped pit. If you don't see its shape, you slide in
and thrash — and thrashing usually digs you deeper. But every pit has a rope (the
way out) hanging in a *specific* spot. You have to recognize the pit to grab the
right rope.

## The eight traps, in plain language

1. **Policy Resistance (fixes that fail).** Everyone yanks the same stock toward
   a different goal; every push provokes an equal counter-push, so nothing moves
   but everyone's exhausted. *Rope:* stop fighting — get everyone in a room and
   find a bigger shared goal. *Example:* prohibition, crash diets.

2. **Tragedy of the Commons.** A shared resource; each user gets the full benefit
   of using it but shares the cost of abusing it — so the feedback is too weak
   and everyone overuses it to ruin. *Rope:* make users feel the cost — regulate
   or privatize access. *Example:* overfishing, a shared "#general" channel
   drowning in noise.

3. **Drift to Low Performance.** You quietly let your standard sag to match
   recent (bad) results, which lowers effort, which lowers results — a downward
   spiral. *Rope:* hold standards **absolute**; anchor to your *best* past, not
   your worst. *Example:* "shipping's always late lately, so late is normal now."

4. **Escalation.** Each side sets its level by trying to beat the other →
   exponential arms race until someone collapses. *Rope:* refuse to compete
   (step out of the loop) or negotiate a cap. *Example:* price wars, discount
   wars, one-upmanship.

5. **Success to the Successful.** Winners get rewarded with the means to win
   again, so they pull further ahead until they take all. *Rope:* level the field
   — diversify, cap the winnings, rewards that don't rig the next round.
   *Example:* the biggest team always gets the best budget/headcount.

6. **Shifting the Burden (addiction/dependence).** A "solution" relieves the
   *symptom* while the real problem festers, and the system's own ability to cope
   atrophies — so it needs the crutch more and more. *Rope:* focus on long-term
   restructuring, not symptom relief; beware anything that hides the real signal.
   *Example:* paid ads masking weak retention; constant firefighting hiding a
   broken process.

7. **Rule Beating.** People obey the *letter* of a rule while defeating its
   *purpose*. *Rope:* treat rule-beating as feedback that the rule is bad —
   redesign it to channel creativity toward the real goal. *Example:* gaming a
   KPI; closing tickets without solving them.

8. **Seeking the Wrong Goal.** The system faithfully hits the target you set —
   but you set the wrong target, so the outcome gets worse. *Rope:* measure real
   welfare, and never confuse **effort** with **result**. *Example:* rewarding
   "signups" while activation dies; "hours worked" instead of value delivered.

## Where your understanding will crack

1. **Blaming people, not structure.** These traps snare *reasonable* actors. If
   swapping the person wouldn't fix it, it's a trap, not a talent problem.
2. **Grabbing the wrong rope.** Escaping "seeking the wrong goal" by *pushing
   harder on the wrong goal* digs deeper. Match the escape to the trap.
3. **Thinking symptom relief = solving.** In "shifting the burden," relief is the
   drug. Feeling better is not the same as being better.
4. **One system, several traps.** A single misused metric can be *both* rule
   beating *and* seeking the wrong goal at once.

## Stress-test questions
1. Support closes tickets fast to hit a target, but customers stay unhappy and
   churn rises. Which *two* traps are tangled here, and what's the escape for
   each?
2. A company is hooked on discounts to hit quarterly numbers; margins erode every
   quarter. Name the trap and the (uncomfortable) way out.
3. Give a "tragedy of the commons" from an *office/online* setting — and describe
   the missing feedback link you'd restore.

## Key lines from the book

  systems understanding, to produce much more desirable behaviors."*
- On seeking the wrong goal: *"Be especially careful not to confuse effort with
  result or you will end up with a system that is producing effort, not result."*
- *"Systems, like the three wishes in the traditional fairy tale, have a terrible
  tendency to produce exactly and only what you ask them to."*
