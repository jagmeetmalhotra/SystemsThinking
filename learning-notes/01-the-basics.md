# Chapter 1 — The Basics

## The one big idea
A system is not a pile of parts — it's **stocks** (amounts) connected by
**flows** (rates), wired together with **feedback loops**. You can't grab the
amount directly; you can only work the taps, and amounts change *slowly*.

## The analogy: a bathtub
- Water in the tub = a **stock** (how much you have right now).
- The faucet = an **inflow**; the drain = an **outflow** (rates, "per minute").
- You can't scoop the water level up with your hand. You raise or lower it
  *only* by turning the taps — and it takes time.

That's the whole chapter in one picture. Everything below is detail.

## Core concepts, plainly

**A system needs three things** (or it's just a heap):
1. **Elements** — the parts (players, ball, coach on a team).
2. **Interconnections** — how they relate (the rules, the passes, information).
3. **A purpose/function** — what it's actually *for*, judged by behavior.
> Change the parts and a system often behaves the same (swap all the players,
> still football). Change the *interconnections or the purpose* and it becomes a
> different system entirely. **So relationships and purpose matter more than
> parts.**

**Stocks are memory.** A stock is "the present memory of the history of its
flows." Your savings account today *is* the record of every deposit and
withdrawal you ever made.

**Stocks move slowly — that's the key to everything.** A stock can only change
as fast as its flows allow. You can't drain a full bathtub in one second, no
matter how wide the drain. This built-in slowness is why systems have momentum,
why quick fixes don't stick, and why patience beats panic.

**Stocks decouple inflow from outflow.** Because a stock sits between the two
taps, inflow and outflow don't have to match moment-to-moment. That gap is what
lets a warehouse hold inventory, a bank hold money, a reservoir hold water — and
also what lets systems drift out of balance.

**Two feedback loops run the show:**
- **Balancing loop (B)** — goal-seeking, stabilizing. Like a thermostat: it
  pushes a stock *toward* a target and resists change. Creates stability.
- **Reinforcing loop (R)** — self-multiplying. Like interest on money or a
  rumor spreading: the more you have, the more you get. Creates growth or
  collapse.

Every dynamic you'll ever see is some mix of these two loops fighting or
cooperating, usually with a delay in between.

## Where your understanding will crack

1. **Confusing a stock with a flow.** "Is *money* a stock or a flow?" Money in
   the account = stock; income = flow (it has a hidden "per month"). Test: if it
   has a time unit baked in, it's a flow.
2. **Expecting instant results.** People cut spending and expect wealth
   tomorrow. The tub doesn't empty the instant you touch the tap. Delay is
   built into every stock.
3. **Fixating on the faucet.** To grow a stock you can raise inflow *or* lower
   outflow. Everyone shouts "get more!" and forgets "stop losing what you have."
4. **Thinking the parts are the system.** Replacing people rarely changes
   behavior; the *structure* (interconnections + purpose) does.
5. **Reducing an inflow ≠ draining the stock.** Turn off the faucet and the
   water already in the tub just *sits there*. To lower a stock you must open a
   drain, not merely slow the fill.

## Stress-test questions (explain in your own words)
1. Your phone's storage is filling up. Name the stock, an inflow, and two
   different ways to free up space — one on each tap.
2. A company says "we grew revenue by hiring more salespeople." Which tap did
   they turn, and what tap might they be ignoring?
3. Why can a system look *perfectly steady* while a huge amount is actually
   flowing through it? (Hint: two taps, same rate.)
