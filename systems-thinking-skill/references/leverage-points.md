# The 12 Leverage Points

> *Places to intervene in a system, in increasing order of effectiveness.*
> — Donella Meadows, *Thinking in Systems*, Ch. 6

Leverage points are places where a small shift produces big changes. They are
**counterintuitive**: people find them reliably, then push in the *wrong*
direction. The list runs from weakest (12) to strongest (1). Most real-world
effort goes into 12–10; most real change comes from 6–1.

Use this to (a) classify every proposed intervention and (b) push the user up
the list.

---

## 12. Numbers — constants & parameters
Subsidies, taxes, standards, budgets, prices, the size of a flow, a quota, a
conversion target. **Lowest leverage.** Diddling with numbers is 99% of
attention and almost never changes behavior — you're adjusting the temperature
of the bathwater, not the plumbing. *Exception:* a number that sets the gain of
a feedback loop or the size of a delay can matter (those are really #8/#9).
- *Your system:* signup target, ad budget, price, SLA threshold, headcount.

## 11. Buffers — the size of stabilizing stocks relative to their flows
A bigger buffer makes a system more stable but less responsive; too big and it
sloshes. Often physical and hard to change.
- *Your system:* cash runway, support staff slack, inventory, onboarding
  capacity headroom.

## 10. Stock-and-flow structures — physical systems & their intersection nodes
The plumbing itself: how stocks connect, where flows split/merge. Huge effect
on behavior, but usually slow/expensive to rebuild (you can't re-pipe overnight).
The leverage is in *understanding* the structure's limits and not expecting a
number to fix a structural bottleneck.
- *Your system:* the actual funnel topology, hand-off path between sub-systems.

## 9. Delays — lengths of time relative to rates of change
Delays in feedback loops are critical. Too-long a delay causes overshoot and
oscillation; you usually *can't* shorten the important ones, and lengthening can
sometimes stabilize. A system that oscillates almost always has a delay problem.
- *Your system:* hiring ramp, word-of-mouth lag, time-to-value in onboarding,
  reporting lag between action and metric.

## 8. Balancing feedback loops — strength relative to the impact they correct
The self-correcting loops that keep a system in bounds (thermostats, immune
responses, regulators). Leverage = making a balancing loop strong enough, fast
enough, and well-enough informed to do its job. Often the missing/weakened loop
*is* the problem.
- *Your system:* quality gates, the loop from unhappy customers back to product,
  capacity-based throttling, guardrail metrics.

## 7. Reinforcing feedback loops — strength of the gain of driving loops
The growth/runaway engines. *Reducing* the gain of a runaway reinforcing loop is
usually higher leverage than strengthening a balancing loop. "Success to the
successful," interest, erosion, and viral growth all live here.
- *Your system:* referral/virality loop, "rich get richer" dynamics, debt/
  churn spirals. Slow a vicious one; nurture a virtuous one — but know it will
  hit a limit.

## 6. Information flows — who does and does not have access to information
Adding or restructuring a feedback of information (to someone who can act on it)
is high leverage and **cheap**. Missing information is the most common cause of
malfunction; delivering the right signal to the right place changes behavior
without force. (Meadows' example: showing households their electricity meter.)
- *Your system:* make the cost/consequence visible to the person whose decision
  causes it; expose churn to the team that creates it; close a reporting gap.

## 5. Rules — incentives, punishments, constraints
The rules define scope, boundaries, degrees of freedom — and who has power over
the rules has real power. Change the incentives and the whole system reorganizes.
- *Your system:* comp/incentive structure, what's rewarded vs. measured,
  policies, contracts, platform terms.

## 4. Self-organization — power to add, change, or evolve system structure
The ability of a system to change *itself* — to add new loops, rules, and
structure (evolution, innovation, learning). The strongest form of resilience.
Leverage = encouraging variability, experimentation, and diversity rather than
"keeping control."
- *Your system:* ability of teams to change their own process, experiment,
  evolve the funnel; protecting the diversity/raw material that lets the system
  adapt.

## 3. Goals — the purpose or function of the whole system
Change the goal and everything below it (rules, loops, flows, parameters) bends
to serve the new goal. A single actor at the top who changes the system's goal
can swing the whole thing. The whole-system goal often differs from what any
part says it's pursuing.
- *Your system:* "maximize signups" vs. "maximize activated, retained value"
  produces entirely different structures. Pick the right goal.

## 2. Paradigms — the mind-set out of which the system arises
The shared, unstated assumptions from which goals, rules, and structure all
flow ("growth is good," "users are funnels," "more features = more value").
Paradigms are the *source* of systems. They can change in an individual in a
millisecond (a click in the mind) yet societies resist fiercely. You shift one
by repeatedly pointing at the old paradigm's anomalies and acting from the new.
- *Your system:* the belief frame behind the metrics — reframe what the system
  is *for*.

## 1. Transcending paradigms — staying unattached to any one paradigm
The highest leverage: realizing that *no* paradigm is "true," staying flexible,
keeping the ability to choose the lens that best serves the purpose at hand.
This is the source of radical empowerment and humility — you stop defending a
worldview and start choosing whichever framing helps.

---

## How to use in the diagnostic
1. List every candidate intervention the analysis suggests.
2. Tag each with its leverage number and a one-line mechanism.
3. Sort high → low. Flag any clustering at 12–10 and ask: *is there a 6/5/3
   move that addresses the same problem at the structure/information/goal level?*
4. Remember Meadows' warning: people find leverage points and push them
   **backwards**. State the *direction* of each push explicitly.
